# Part 4 — Socratic Strategy Questions
*By observing the past, we test the future. By knowing others, we know ourselves.*

1)  **Fed stance:** Your positioning seems to assume the Fed is on a sustained, data-dependent pause, similar to the successful soft-landing navigation of late 1994. Is it worth testing the alternative—that this is a temporary pause before a final hawkish push, akin to the "autopilot" error of late 2018?
    - **Test:** Monitor the median dot plot projection in the next Summary of Economic Projections and the language of three or more voting FOMC members over the next 45 days.
    - **Falsifier:** If the median 2026 dot plot remains at or below the current FFR and no more than one voting member uses the phrase "further tightening may be needed," the 1994 view holds.

2)  **Optimism quality:** The market's resilience implies a belief in genuine macro strength, as in mid-2005 when the economy absorbed rate hikes. How do we stress-test the possibility that this is misplaced optimism vulnerable to a credit shock, like the denial phase of mid-2007?
    - **Test:** Track the CDX Investment Grade and High Yield indices over the FP-R window.
    - **Falsifier:** If the HY-IG spread fails to widen by more than 25 bps during FP-R, the "genuine strength" hypothesis is intact.

3)  **Single decisive indicator:** We seem to be implicitly weighting the inflation trajectory as the most decisive indicator for the next market regime. Is it possible that, with inflation stabilizing, the decisive indicator has shifted to either labor strength (the Fed's other mandate) or credit conditions (the transmission mechanism)?
    - **Test:** Compare the market's reaction (beta) to the next NFP report, the next CPI report, and the next Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS).
    - **Falsifier:** If the S&P 500's absolute 1-day move is greatest in response to the CPI print, our current focus on inflation remains correct.
