*By observing the past, we test the future. By knowing others, we know ourselves.*

We ask these because history shows they are decisive in similar regimes. Answering them clarifies assumptions and improves decisions:
1) **Fed stance:** Your process seems to assume the Fed is operating like it was in late-1994, ready to pause based on incoming data. How do we test this against the late-2018 "autopilot" hawkishness? A simple test is to observe the official reaction to the next soft employment or inflation print. **Falsifier:** If a key report (e.g., NFP) misses expectations but two or more Fed governors still emphasize "ongoing" or "further" policy firming within 3 trading days, the 2018 analog gains significant weight.

2) **Optimism quality:** The AI-driven optimism feels strong, but is it genuine macro strength (mid-2005) or a narrow, vulnerable pivot-bet (late-2007)? We can measure its breadth by comparing tech leadership against cyclical sectors during the FP-R window. A test is the ratio of the Industrials ETF (XLI) to the Nasdaq 100 ETF (QQQ). **Falsifier:** If the XLI/QQQ ratio makes new 3-month lows during FP-R, it suggests optimism is dangerously narrow, mirroring the 2007 setup.

3) **Single decisive indicator:** The market appears keyed on inflation as the primary variable. But history shows credit conditions can sometimes move first and with more force. To test whether inflation is still the lead indicator, we can watch for signals of stress in funding markets. **Falsifier:** If the FRA-OIS spread (a measure of bank funding stress) widens by more than 10 bps before the next CPI release, credit has likely seized control from inflation as the market's primary driver.
